Arizona Water Rights (The "Deep End")

⚠️
Exam Trap: Know the difference between Type 1 and Type 2 Grandfathered Rights.
Groundwater Management Act (1980)

Created AMAs (Active Management Areas) where overdraft is severe. (Phoenix, Tucson, Prescott, Pinal, Santa Cruz).

Right Type Definition Transferability
Irrigation Grandfathered Farming use. Runs with the land.
Type 1 (Non-Irrigation) Retired farm land converted to non-farm use (e.g., Golf Course). Runs with the land. Cannot be sold separately.
Type 2 (Non-Irrigation) Historical non-farm use (e.g., Mines, Dairies). Portable. Can be sold separately from the land.
Assured vs. Adequate Water Supply
  • Inside an AMA: Developers must prove a 100-Year Assured Water Supply to subdivide.
  • Outside an AMA: Developers must prove an Adequate Water Supply. If inadequate, they can still build, but must disclose it in the Public Report.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Riparian vs Littoral Water Rights

Both are water rights based on property location — know the difference:

Feature 🌊 Riparian Rights 🏖️ Littoral Rights
Water Type FLOWING water STANDING water
Examples Rivers, streams, creeks Lakes, ponds, oceans, seas
Ownership Extends To Center of waterway (if non-navigable) High-water mark
Usage Rights Reasonable use that doesn't harm downstream owners Reasonable use of water touching property
Memory Trick Riparian = River (flows) Littoral = Lake (sits)
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Accretion vs Avulsion vs Erosion

All involve land changes from water — timing is key:

Term What Happens Speed Who Owns New Land?
Accretion Land is ADDED by water deposit (soil buildup) GRADUAL Property owner gains it
Reliction Land is EXPOSED by water receding GRADUAL Property owner gains it
Erosion Land is LOST by water washing away GRADUAL Property owner loses it
Avulsion Land is TORN AWAY suddenly (flood, earthquake) SUDDEN Original owner may reclaim

Memory Tip: AVulsion = Violent/Sudden. Everything else is gradual.

Subdivision & Land Rules

The "Magic Numbers"
Term Definition Requirement
Subdivision Dividing land into 6 or more parcels (each < 36 acres). Must obtain a Public Report from ADRE.
Unsubdivided Land Dividing land into 6 or more parcels (each 36-160 acres). Must obtain a Public Report.
Lot Split Dividing land into 5 or fewer parcels. No Public Report. Requires Affidavit of Disclosure if in county island.
Public Report (The CPR)

Commissioner's Public Report. Protects the buyer.

  • Must be given to buyer before signing the contract.
  • If buyer signs without receiving it, the sale is Voidable by the buyer for 3 years.
  • Lot Reservations: Developer can take $5,000 max deposit before Public Report is issued (fully refundable).

Classification Number of Lots Lot Size Requirements

Subdivision (Improved) 6 or more lots Less than 36 acres each Public Report REQUIRED

Exempt (Large Parcels) Any number Over 160 acres each NO Public Report required
Public Report Types
Report Type What It Allows Requirements
Preliminary Public Report Take refundable reservations only (max $5,000) Before lots are ready for sale

Special Order of Exemption Sale without Public Report Specific criteria met; ADRE approval
Affidavit of Disclosure (Lot Splits)

Required when selling unimproved land in unincorporated areas (county islands):

  • Must disclose: road maintenance, utilities, easements, zoning, flood zone
  • Must be given to buyer before purchase agreement
  • Filed with county recorder after sale
  • Applies to parcels 5 or fewer (lot splits, not subdivisions)
⚠️
Exam Trap — The Magic Number 6: Once you hit 6 or more lots, you need a Public Report! 5 or fewer = lot split (no Public Report). Remember: "5 is FINE, 6 needs the FIX (Public Report)!"
🎯
Exam Tip — 36 Acres: Under 36 acres = subdivision. Between 36-160 acres = unsubdivided land. Over 160 acres = exempt. Both subdivision AND unsubdivided land need a Public Report if 6+ lots!
💡
Memory Tip — Public Report Voidable: If buyer doesn't receive Public Report before signing, sale is VOIDABLE for 3 YEARS. "CPR saves lives — give it FIRST!"
⚠️ Unimproved Lot Rescission — A.R.S. § 32-2185.01

Arizona provides extra protection when buyer purchases an UNIMPROVED subdivision lot without physically inspecting it:

Situation Rescission Period Details
Buyer inspected lot BEFORE signing NO rescission right Standard contract rules apply
Buyer did NOT inspect lot before signing 7 CALENDAR DAYS to rescind OR Can rescind without cause within 7 days
Buyer did NOT inspect lot before signing 6 MONTHS to inspect then rescind Has 6 months to inspect; can rescind after inspection
IMPROVED lots (with building) NO rescission right These rules only apply to UNIMPROVED lots

Key Points:

  • Only applies to UNIMPROVED lots (no building)
  • Only applies if buyer did NOT inspect BEFORE signing
  • Developer must refund ALL money upon rescission
  • Cannot waive this right
Lot Reservation Deposits (Before Public Report)
Rule Details
Maximum Deposit $5,000 maximum (per A.R.S. § 32-2181.03)
Refundable? 100% REFUNDABLE at any time before Public Report issued
Where Held? Must be placed in a trust account or escrow
When Can Be Used? Only with Preliminary Public Report (not before any report)
Binding Contract? NO — reservation only, NOT a purchase contract
⚠️
Exam Trap — Unimproved Lot Rescission: If buyer purchases an UNIMPROVED subdivision lot WITHOUT inspecting it first, they get 7 CALENDAR DAYS to cancel outright, OR 6 MONTHS to inspect then cancel. If they DID inspect before signing — NO rescission right!
🎯
Exam Tip — Reservation vs Contract: Before Full Public Report, developer can only take refundable reservations (not binding contracts). Think: "No CPR = No Commitment!"

Arizona Consumer Protection Laws

Real Estate Recovery Fund

Administered by ADRE to compensate consumers harmed by licensed agents:

  • Maximum per transaction: $30,000
  • Maximum per licensee: $90,000 total
  • Requirements: Must have final court judgment against licensee
  • Coverage: Actual out-of-pocket losses only (no speculative damages)
  • Time limit: Claim must be filed within 5 years

Note: If fund pays out, licensee's license is typically terminated.

Arizona Consumer Fraud Protection Act (1967)

A.R.S. §§ 44-1521 — Applies to real estate transactions:

  • Prohibits: Deception, fraud, misrepresentation, concealment of material facts
  • Applies to: All RE sales, advertising, and written/verbal statements
  • Private Action: Consumers can sue; easier to prove than common law fraud
  • Penalties: Civil penalties, restitution, potentially treble damages
Arizona Homestead Exemption

A.R.S. § 33-1101 — Protects home equity from general creditors:

Feature Details
Protected Amount (2025) $425,200 equity (adjusted annually for cost of living since Prop 209 passed in 2022)
Acreage Limit NO acreage limit — Must be "one compact body" with dwelling
Eligible Property Types
  • House on land (any acreage, one compact parcel)
  • Condominium unit
  • Cooperative unit
  • Mobile home (with or without land)
Residency Requirement Must be your PRIMARY residence where you actually live
Age Requirement Must be 18+ years old and Arizona resident
Number of Exemptions Only ONE per person (married couple = one shared exemption)
Automatic? Yes — no filing required (but can file Declaration for extra protection)
Sale Proceeds Protected for 18 months after sale (used for new home)
Does NOT Protect Against
  • Mortgage/deed of trust foreclosure
  • Mechanic's liens (for work on the home)
  • Child support & alimony
  • Property taxes
  • HOA liens
⚠️
Don't Confuse: Homestead (no acreage limit) vs. Anti-Deficiency (2.5 acre limit). These are DIFFERENT protections with different rules!
Arizona Anti-Deficiency Statute

Protects homeowners from "deficiency judgments" after foreclosure:

Requirement Details
Property Size 2.5 acres or less
Property Type Single 1-family or 2-family dwelling
Trustee Sale (Non-Judicial) Protection applies regardless of loan type
Judicial Foreclosure Protection ONLY for "purchase money" loans
📋
Purchase Money Loan = Loan used specifically to PURCHASE the property (not HELOCs or refinances for cash-out)
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Homestead vs Anti-Deficiency — Know the Difference!

These are commonly confused on the exam. Here's how they differ:

Feature 🏠 Homestead Exemption 🛡️ Anti-Deficiency Statute
Purpose Protects home EQUITY from general creditors Protects from DEFICIENCY JUDGMENT after foreclosure
Acreage Limit NO LIMIT — Any size property 2.5 ACRES OR LESS
Property Type House, condo, co-op, mobile home Single 1-family or 2-family dwelling ONLY
Dollar Limit $425,200 (2025, adjusted annually) UNLIMITED — Protects entire deficiency
When It Applies When creditors try to collect debts (lawsuits, judgments) ONLY after a foreclosure sale
Residency Required? YES — Must be your primary residence NO — Applies even to investment property*
Filing Required? No — automatic (but can file declaration) No — automatic if qualifications met
Statute Reference A.R.S. § 33-1101 A.R.S. § 33-729(A) & § 33-814(G)

*Anti-deficiency for trustee sale applies regardless of occupancy; for judicial foreclosure, must be purchase money loan.

⚠️ Arizona Eviction Process (Forcible Detainer)

Arizona uses Special Detainer action for evictions — much faster than regular civil cases:

Step Action Timeframe
1. Proper Notice Serve appropriate written notice (5-day, 10-day, etc.) Required BEFORE filing
2. File Action After notice expires, file eviction in Justice Court Day after notice period ends
3. Hearing Court hearing scheduled 3-6 days after filing
4. Judgment If landlord wins, immediate judgment for possession Same day as hearing
5. Writ of Restitution Court issues order for constable to remove tenant 5 calendar days after judgment
6. Lock-Out Constable supervises lock change; tenant removed Typically 12-24 hours after writ
🚫
ILLEGAL: Self-help eviction! Landlord CANNOT change locks, shut off utilities, or remove tenant's belongings without court order. Penalty: 2x damages!

Fiduciary Duties — OLD CAR

Agents owe these duties to their CLIENT (not customers):

Duty Description Example
Obedience Follow client's lawful instructions Do not show home on Sundays if client requests
Loyalty Put client's interests above your own Don't steer to properties that pay higher commission
Disclosure Share ALL material facts with client Report every offer, even unpleasant ones
Confidentiality Keep client information private FOREVER Don't reveal seller's urgency to buyer
Accounting Track all funds and documents Deposit earnest money, provide receipts
Reasonable Care Perform duties competently Verify facts before sharing, complete contracts correctly
⚠️
Exam Trap — Confidentiality is FOREVER! Even after the agency relationship ends, you cannot reveal confidential information about your former client to anyone.

Types of Listing Agreements

A listing agreement is an employment contract between a seller and a broker that authorizes the broker to find a buyer. There are four main types:

1. Exclusive Right to Sell ⭐ (Most Common)

  • Definition: Only ONE broker is authorized to market the property
  • Commission: Broker gets paid if property sells — no matter who sells it
  • Who it benefits: The BROKER (guarantees commission)
  • Key Point: Seller pays even if they find their own buyer

2. Exclusive Agency

  • Definition: ONE broker is authorized, but seller retains right to sell independently
  • Commission: Broker gets paid only if broker or cooperating broker sells
  • Who it benefits: More balanced — seller keeps FSBO option
  • Key Point: NO commission if seller finds buyer themselves

3. Open Listing (Non-Exclusive)

  • Definition: Seller may list with MULTIPLE brokers simultaneously
  • Commission: Only the broker who actually procures the buyer gets paid
  • Who it benefits: The SELLER (multiple agents competing)
  • Key Point: Sometimes called "Give it to everybody" listing
  • Termination: Terminates when ANY broker sells the property

4. Net Listing ⚠️

  • Definition: Seller sets a "net" price; broker keeps anything above that amount
  • Commission: Whatever exceeds the seller's desired net amount
  • Who it benefits: Potentially exploits sellers
  • Status: LEGAL in Arizona but strongly discouraged
  • Key Point: Creates conflict of interest — broker motivated to underprice

Proration Calculations

Proration Basics

Prorations divide ongoing expenses between buyer and seller based on days of ownership.

Method Calculation When Used
365-Day Year Annual Amount ÷ 365 = Daily Rate Most accurate; "actual days"
360-Day Year (Banker's Year) Annual Amount ÷ 360 = Daily Rate 12 months × 30 days (simplified)
30-Day Month Monthly Amount ÷ 30 = Daily Rate Quick monthly calculations
Common Proration Items
Item Typically Paid Proration Rule
Property Taxes Arrears (after due) Seller DEBITED for days owned
Prepaid Rent In advance Seller CREDITED for buyer's days
HOA Dues Varies Depends on when due
Interest Arrears Seller DEBITED through closing day
Insurance In advance Usually NOT prorated (buyer gets new policy)
Proration Formula
  1. Find Daily Rate: Annual Amount ÷ 365 (or 360)
  2. Count Days: From Jan 1 (or last payment) to closing date
  3. Calculate Amount: Daily Rate × Number of Days
  4. Assign to Party:
    • Paid in ARREARS: Seller owes → Debit Seller, Credit Buyer
    • Paid in ADVANCE: Seller prepaid → Credit Seller, Debit Buyer
📅
Closing Day Rule: Typically the buyer owns the DAY OF CLOSING and pays for that day!
📝 Proration Practice Problem (Click to reveal)
Problem: Annual property taxes are $3,650. Closing is March 15. Taxes are paid in arrears. Using a 365-day year, how much does the seller owe?
Solution:
1. Daily rate: $3,650 ÷ 365 = $10/day
2. Days seller owned: Jan (31) + Feb (28) + March 1-14 (14) = 73 days
3. Seller owes: 73 × $10 = $730
Seller is DEBITED $730; Buyer is CREDITED $730

📝 Advanced Practice Problems (Depreciation & Investment)
13. DEPRECIATION: A building cost $500,000 to construct. Land value is $100,000. The IRS allows 27.5-year depreciation for residential rentals. What is the annual depreciation deduction?
Depreciable basis = $500,000 - $100,000 = $400,000
Annual depreciation: $400,000 ÷ 27.5 = $14,545.45/year
Note: Only improvements depreciate, NOT land!
14. DEPRECIATION (Commercial): A commercial building is worth $1,200,000. Land is $200,000. Using 39-year straight-line depreciation, what is the annual deduction?
Depreciable basis = $1,200,000 - $200,000 = $1,000,000
Annual depreciation: $1,000,000 ÷ 39 = $25,641.03/year
15. DCR (Debt Coverage Ratio): Property has NOI of $75,000. Annual debt service is $60,000. What is the DCR? Is the lender likely to approve?
DCR = NOI ÷ Debt Service = $75,000 ÷ $60,000 = 1.25
Yes, lender will likely approve. Most lenders require DCR ≥ 1.20 (income exceeds debt payments by 20%+)
16. CASH-ON-CASH RETURN: Investor puts $50,000 down on a rental. After all expenses and mortgage payments, annual cash flow is $6,000. What is the cash-on-cash return?
Cash-on-Cash = Annual Cash Flow ÷ Cash Invested
$6,000 ÷ $50,000 = 0.12 = 12% cash-on-cash return
17. APPRECIATION: A home was purchased for $250,000 and is now worth $325,000. What is the percentage of appreciation?
Gain = $325,000 - $250,000 = $75,000
Appreciation = $75,000 ÷ $250,000 = 0.30 = 30% appreciation

🤝 Client (Agent) vs Customer — Duties Owed in Arizona
Duty CLIENT (Full Fiduciary - OLD CAR) CUSTOMER (Limited Duties)
Relationship Agency relationship exists (signed agreement or implied) NO agency — licensee works for someone else
Obedience ✅ Must obey all lawful instructions ❌ Not owed
Loyalty ✅ Put client's interests FIRST ❌ Not owed — loyalty is to the client (other party)
Disclosure ✅ Disclose ALL known material facts ⚠️ LIMITED — must disclose known material defects that affect value
Confidentiality ✅ Keep bargaining info confidential (even after relationship ends) ❌ Not owed
Accounting ✅ Account for all money/property ⚠️ If handling funds, must account
Reasonable Care ✅ Use skill and expertise to assist ⚠️ Must be honest and accurate
Deal Fairly ✅ Yes ✅ YES — must deal fairly with ALL parties
Disclose Material Defects ✅ Yes ✅ YES — must disclose known defects to all parties
🎯
EXAM CRITICAL — Customer Duties:
Even when you DON'T represent someone, you STILL owe customers:
• Deal fairly with all parties
• Disclose material facts that adversely affect value
• Be honest and accurate
• Disclose known defects
❌ You do NOT owe: Obedience, Loyalty, or Confidentiality to customers!
💡
EXAMPLE — When is someone a CUSTOMER?
Scenario: You are a listing agent representing the SELLER. A buyer calls to see the home but does NOT have their own agent and has NOT signed a buyer representation agreement with you.

The BUYER is your CUSTOMER:
• You represent the SELLER (your client) — full OLD CAR duties to seller
• The BUYER is a customer — you owe limited duties only
• You MUST deal fairly, disclose defects, be honest
• You do NOT owe the buyer loyalty, obedience, or confidentiality
• If the buyer tells you "I'll pay $10K more if needed" — you CAN tell your client (seller)!
📋 Transaction Broker Note (Arizona)

Arizona does NOT have a statutory "Transaction Broker" status like some other states. In Arizona, you are either:

  • An Agent — Full fiduciary duties (OLD CAR) to your CLIENT
  • Not an Agent — Limited duties to the CUSTOMER (other party)

Some states allow "transaction brokers" who facilitate but don't represent either party. This is NOT recognized in Arizona statute.

⚠️
EXAM TIP: If exam asks about "transaction broker" in Arizona context, remember AZ uses CLIENT/CUSTOMER model, not transaction broker model.

Void vs Voidable vs Unenforceable
Type Meaning Examples Key Point
VOID NEVER valid — no legal effect AT ALL • Contract to sell illegal drugs
• Contract signed by declared incompetent person
• Contract for illegal purpose (gambling where prohibited)
• Forged deed 		Cannot be ratified or fixed — it's as if it never existed
VOIDABLE Valid UNTIL the injured party chooses to void it • Minor signs contract (can void when turns 18)
• Contract signed under duress/threat
• Contract signed due to fraud/misrepresentation

⚠️
EXAM TIP: "VOID = NEVER valid (think: AVOID completely!). VOIDABLE = Valid until VOIDED by one party."
💡
EXAMPLE — Void vs Voidable:

VOIDABLE (Can be ratified):
A 17-year-old signs a contract to buy a car. At 17, the contract is VOIDABLE (minor can cancel it). When they turn 18, they can either:
• Ratify it — keep the car and the contract becomes fully valid
• Void it — cancel and return the car

VOID (Cannot be ratified):
A person declared legally incompetent by a court signs a deed. This deed is VOID — it has NO legal effect and CANNOT be fixed, even if the person later regains competency.

Key: VOIDABLE = one party has a CHOICE. VOID = NO choice, it never existed legally.

📝 When is a Contract BINDING? (Signed vs Recorded)
Stage What Happens Is Contract Binding?
Offer Made Buyer submits written offer with earnest money ❌ NO — just an offer
Seller Signs Seller signs the acceptance ❌ NO — acceptance must be COMMUNICATED
Acceptance DELIVERED Signed acceptance is delivered to buyer or buyer's agent ✅ YES — CONTRACT IS BINDING!
Closing (Executed) All obligations performed, deed transferred ✅ Binding (and now complete)
Recording Deed recorded with county recorder N/A — Recording is for PUBLIC NOTICE, not to make contract binding
🚨
CRITICAL EXAM DISTINCTIONS:
• Contract is binding when acceptance is SIGNED + DELIVERED to other party
• Recording is NOT required for a valid contract — it's for priority/public notice
• If seller signs but doesn't communicate acceptance → NO binding contract yet!
• "Executing" a contract = signing it (makes it executory)
• "Executed" contract = all obligations complete (at closing)
📚 Note: These are GENERAL CONTRACT LAW principles that apply nationally (including Arizona), not AZ-specific rules.
💡
HOW is Acceptance Communicated?
Acceptance can be delivered by any reasonable method, including:
• In person — hand delivery of signed contract
• Email — electronic delivery (most common today)
• Fax — still valid method
• Text message — if agreed upon as acceptable
• Through agent — seller's agent delivers to buyer's agent
• Mail — regular or certified mail

Key Points:
• The method should be what both parties agreed to or what's reasonable
• Acceptance is effective when RECEIVED by the other party (not when sent)
• If seller signs but leaves it in their drawer → NOT communicated → NO contract!
Bilateral vs Unilateral Contracts
Feature BILATERAL Contract UNILATERAL Contract
Definition BOTH parties make promises and are obligated ONE party makes a promise; other party CAN perform but isn't obligated
Who is Bound? Both buyer AND seller must perform Only ONE party is bound until the other performs
Real Estate Examples • Purchase Agreement (buyer promises to pay, seller promises to convey)
• Listing Agreement (broker promises to market, seller promises to pay commission IF sold) 		• OPTION CONTRACT (seller must sell if buyer exercises; buyer is NOT obligated to buy)
• Open Listing (seller only pays if that specific broker sells)
Memory Tip "BI = TWO = Both obligated" "UNI = ONE = One side obligated"
🎯
🚨 EXAM FAVORITE: An OPTION CONTRACT is UNILATERAL!
• The optionor (seller) is BOUND to sell at the agreed price during the option period
• The optionee (buyer) is NOT obligated to buy — they have the RIGHT but not the DUTY
• Option consideration is typically non-refundable
• If buyer exercises the option → becomes a BILATERAL purchase contract
⚖️ Contract Remedies & Termination
Remedy/Action Definition When Used
Specific Performance Court ORDERS party to perform the contract (not money damages) Real estate is UNIQUE — buyer can force seller to sell because no two properties are alike
Monetary Damages Money compensation for breach When specific performance isn't appropriate or desired
Liquidated Damages Pre-agreed amount (usually earnest money) if buyer defaults Seller keeps earnest money if buyer backs out without cause
Rescission Contract is cancelled and parties returned to original position Mutual agreement, fraud discovered, or contingency not met
Novation New contract replaces old; original party is RELEASED from liability New buyer takes over contract; original buyer is off the hook
Assignment Transfer contract rights to another; original party REMAINS liable Investor assigns purchase contract but stays responsible
Assumption New buyer takes over existing loan; original borrower may stay liable FHA/VA loans may be assumable with lender approval
🎯
EXAM TRAPS:
• NOVATION vs ASSIGNMENT: Novation = RELEASED, Assignment = STILL LIABLE
• Specific Performance works for BUYERS forcing sellers (property is unique)
• Sellers typically seek liquidated damages (keep earnest money) rather than specific performance
💡
EXAMPLE — Novation vs Assignment:
Scenario: Seller has a mortgage with Bank ABC. Seller sells to Buyer.

ASSIGNMENT: Buyer takes over payments but original Seller is still on the hook. If Buyer defaults, Bank can sue Seller!
NOVATION: Bank releases Seller completely and creates NEW loan agreement with Buyer only. Seller is FREE and CLEAR.

Key: Novation requires ALL 3 parties to agree (Seller, Buyer, AND Bank). Assignment only needs 2.
📝 Listing Agreement Types
Listing Type Commission Earned When... Key Feature

⚠️
EXAM TIP: On the exam, if a seller sold their own property:
• Exclusive Right to Sell = Broker STILL gets paid ✅
• Exclusive Agency = Broker gets NOTHING ❌
• Open Listing = Broker gets NOTHING ❌
💡
EXAMPLE — Seller Sells Their Own Property:
Scenario: Seller signs listing with Broker ABC. Later, Seller's coworker offers to buy directly — no broker involved.

If Exclusive Right to Sell: Broker ABC is STILL owed commission! ✅ (Broker gets paid no matter WHO finds the buyer)
If Exclusive Agency: Broker ABC gets NOTHING ❌ (Seller found buyer themselves)
If Open Listing: Broker ABC gets NOTHING ❌ (Seller found buyer themselves)

Key: Exclusive Right to Sell = BEST protection for broker. This is why it's the most common listing!
